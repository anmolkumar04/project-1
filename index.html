V-Type Engine

A V‑type engine is an internal‑combustion engine whose cylinders are split into two banks set at an angle (typically about 60–90 degrees) around a single crankshaft, forming a “V” shape. Key advantages include a shorter overall length than an equivalent inline engine, the ability to package more cylinders in a compact space, and (in some layouts) excellent smoothness and balance such as the inherently well‑balanced V12.

What it is

In a V engine, two rows of cylinders share one crankshaft; the “included angle” between the banks defines the V and varies widely by design and application. Common production angles cluster near 60–90 degrees because they package well and work with typical firing orders and crankshaft designs.

Advantages

  • Shorter length than an equivalent inline engine, which helps fit larger or more cylinders where engine bay length is limited.
  • Allows higher cylinder counts (V6, V8, V12) in a compact package, supporting higher power potential for performance and luxury vehicles as well as trucks and SUVs.
  • Certain V configurations offer very smooth operation; notably, a V12 has inherently excellent primary and secondary balance for refined running.
  • Compared with a flat (boxer) engine, a V is narrower, which can aid packaging in width‑constrained compartments even though it is typically taller.

Types​

  • V2 (V‑twin): Widely used in motorcycles; the earliest automotive V‑twin dates back to 1889 Daimler applications<< /li>
  • V3: Rare configuration occasionally seen in motorcycles and discussed in overview lists of V layouts
  • V4: Uncommon; used in some bikes and marine outboards due to packaging needs.
  • V5: Very rare automotive layout; appears in configuration roundups but far less common than V6/V8
  • V6: Most common modern car V engine; typical bank angles include 60°, 90°, or 120° depending on design goals
  • V8: Very common in performance, luxury, trucks; often uses a 90° bank angle for even firing with common crank designs.
  • V10: Less common; used in high‑performance road cars and some diesels, with notable adoption in models like the Dodge Viper historically.​
  • V12: Premium/performance applications; commonly built at 60° and valued for smoothness and refinement

    V6 engine

    "V6" redirects here. For other uses, see V6 (disambiguation).
    A V6 engine is a six-cylinder piston engine where the cylinders share a common crankshaft and are arranged in a V configuration.

    V8 engine

    "V8" and "Small block" redirect here. For other uses, see V8 (disambiguation), V8 engine (disambiguation), and Small block (disambiguation).
    A V8 engine is an eight-cylinder piston engine where the cylinders share a common crankshaft and are arranged in a V configuration.

    V12 engine

    A V12 engine is a twelve-cylinder piston engine where two banks of six cylinders are arranged in a V configuration around a common crankshaft. V12 engines are more common than V10 engines. However, they are less common than V8 engines.
